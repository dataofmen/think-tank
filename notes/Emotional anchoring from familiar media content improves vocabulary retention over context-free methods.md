The claim draws on context-dependent memory theory from cognitive psychology: information encoded alongside rich emotional and situational context is recalled more easily than decontextualized material. Vocabulary extracted from a TV series a learner enjoys carries emotional anchors (character relationships, plot tension, humor) that generic vocabulary lists lack.

However, this specific system lacks controlled experimental data comparing retention rates. The claim is an interpretation based on established cognitive science principles applied to a novel delivery mechanism. Two boundary conditions weaken it: (1) studying cards without watching the series strips the emotional context, and (2) genre-specific vocabulary (fantasy, sci-fi) may have limited everyday applicability.

The pipeline design mitigates these risks by recommending a "cards first, then episode" workflow that preserves contextual connection.
